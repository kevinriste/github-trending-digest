Analysis

Auto polo was a short-lived, high-impact motorsport popular in the United States and Europe between 1911 and the late 1920s that adapted the rules of equestrian polo for modified Ford Model T automobiles. Conceived by car dealer Ralph "Pappy" Hankinson as a publicity stunt to drive vehicle sales, the sport featured drivers and mallet-wielding passengers competing in arenas while attempting to strike a basketball toward opposing goals. The game was notoriously dangerous and expensive, frequently resulting in demolished vehicles and significant injuries due to frequent high-speed collisions. Ultimately, the high cost of mechanical maintenance and the extreme physical risks to participants led to the sport's decline, serving as a historical example of early 20th-century experiential marketing and stunt-based entertainment.
